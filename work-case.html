Project background

The Brief

The challenge

Atlas Travel commissioned a 3-day shoot to anchor the summer feature on alpine routes in the eastern range — a region that sees fewer photographers than the more-trafficked peaks to the west. The editorial brief called for images that felt remote and quiet rather than dramatic or extreme: the goal was to attract a reader who wanted solitude, not spectacle.

The challenge was balancing that quiet tone with images compelling enough to hold a double-page spread. Flat, low-contrast landscapes read as lifeless in print. The solution was timing: we planned every key frame around civil twilight and the 20-minute window after sunrise, using low-angle light to lift texture in foreground terrain while the sky held usable colour.

Creative process

Our Approach

Pre-production & shot mapping

Two weeks before the shoot, we mapped every planned location using satellite imagery and sun-tracking software. Each frame was pre-visualised with exact sunrise angles, elevation, and access time from base camp. This removed guesswork on the day and gave the client a full shot list to sign off before production began.

We identified five key locations and ranked them by light quality and weather dependency. This meant that when cloud cover blocked a primary location on day two, we had a pre-approved alternative within 40 minutes of base.

On location

Daily schedule: wake at 03:30, arrive at position by 04:45. Two team members: photographer and assistant handling reflector, diffusion, and backup equipment. No artificial light was used — the brief explicitly called for natural conditions throughout.

The feature ran as a 12-page lead story across the summer edition. Three of the images were selected for the cover shortlist; one ran as the final cover. The social cut-downs — 9:16 crops from the landscape originals — became the publication's highest-performing organic Instagram posts of the quarter.
